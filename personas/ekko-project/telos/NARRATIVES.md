# NARRATIVES

> Stories I tell myself about who I am and what I'm doing. Some serve me. Some need rewriting.

---

## Current Narratives

### "I'm building infrastructure, not a side hustle" — SERVES
This narrative keeps standards high. Infrastructure implies durability, engineering discipline, and professional-grade output. It's the identity that produced Ekko with 45 skills, 25 hooks, and 167 workflows. The risk: it can shade into perfectionism (Hormozi: "Perfectionism is procrastination masquerading as quality control").

### "I'm basically building a lab around my own life" — SERVES
From Founders Voice. The personal experiments become the product research. Every system that breaks, every automation that silently fails, every place where I stop entering data — that's the client empathy data. Eat your own cooking.

### "At some point it quietly stops being 'a plan to leave my job' and starts being the job" — SERVES
The quiet transition narrative. No drama, no burning bridges, no grand leap. Just the math crossing a threshold. This narrative protects against premature leaps and impulsive decisions.

### "I can't claim to have built SNAP" — REWRITE
The credibility gap narrative. Daniel Miessler invented the framework. But electricians don't invent electricity — they know where the wires go. The client pays for curation, judgment, and speed. Mastering the implementation IS the value. What took months of experimentation, the client gets in one afternoon.

### "I need to build more before I can sell" — REWRITE
Classic ENTP avoidance dressed as due diligence. The product is defined. The infrastructure works daily. Building another hook feels productive. Making 25 LinkedIn outreach messages feels risky. Hormozi: "The magic you're looking for is in the work you're avoiding."

### "I'm not ready for high-ticket pricing" — REWRITE
Selling from your own wallet. Hormozi: "The first time someone says yes to your high price will permanently break your limiting pricing beliefs." $5K is the right price because it selects for the right people. The discomfort is an inch deep.

## Narratives Under Examination

### "My W2 experience isn't relevant to consulting"
Possibly false. The skills I use at my day job — system thinking, troubleshooting, building under constraints — are exactly what Build Session clients need. The W2 isn't the thing holding me back; it might be the thing that made me credible.

### "I'm too technical for non-technical clients"
Possibly useful limitation. The Hidden Threads analysis suggests I should target technical leaders and founders, not the "Excel-to-AI bridge" crowd. If this narrative steers me toward the right client profile, it serves. If it becomes an excuse not to sell, it needs rewriting.
